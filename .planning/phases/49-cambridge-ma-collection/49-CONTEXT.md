<decisions>
## Implementation Decisions

### Topic scope and balance
- City-government heavy: Cambridge City Council, City Manager structure, city services, ordinances, civic institutions
- Deep Cambridge history is the backbone — not modern political coverage
- Modern civic leaders captured mainly through expiring questions (see below)
- Don't focus on companies or universities (Harvard, MIT) as topics — their civic non-profit work may be eligible, but the collection is not an ad for either institution
- Include Cambridge's distinctive government structures as part of the mix (not a cornerstone): PR voting system, City Manager form, 9-member at-large Council

### Expiring vs. durable question mix
- Target roughly half-and-half: ~50% expiring + ~50% durable
- Expiring: "Who is the current City Manager?" — designed to be refreshed after elections/appointments
- Durable: structural, historical, and civic facts that don't change
- Pair expiring questions with durable counterparts in the same topic area

### Difficulty calibration
- Over-index on easy questions — Easy Steps design scheme requires easy questions until player gets 2 right
- "Easy" = more people would know the answer (not address or phone number trivia)
- **Never use questions whose answer is an address or phone number**
- Apply the dinner party test: is this a tidbit a civic-minded person would be proud to share? If yes, it belongs. If it feels like admin trivia, cut it.

### Anti-partisan framing
- Platform is actively anti-partisan (not just non-partisan) — strip partisanship from questions entirely
- Avoid questions framed around controversial ballot outcomes, party affiliations, or endorsements
- Focus on civic structures, historical facts, and what leaders *did* in their civic roles — not political positioning
- Goal: an experienced player leaves with genuine understanding of Cambridge, not a partisan lens

### "Feel seen" standard
- The collection should represent the whole city: long-time working families (East Cambridge, Cambridgeport), Portuguese and Haitian immigrant communities, housing advocates, civic activists, and all Cambridge residents — not just the university-adjacent population
- This is a values principle, not audience targeting — the same pride we'd feel if a West Monroe, LA player felt seen applies here

### Must-include civic facts
- Cambridge was the first US city to pass a living wage ordinance (1998) — quintessential dinner party tidbit
- Cambridge abolished PR voting in 1938 under controversy, then restored it in 1941 — a remarkable civic reversal worth multiple questions
- Cambridge Rindge and Latin School (CRLS) — one of the most diverse high schools in the country, with its own civic history

### Source strategy
- Prioritize reputable sources:
  - **Official**: Cambridge city website, City Council meeting records, Cambridge Community Development
  - **Local journalism**: Cambridge Day, Cambridge Chronicle (historical), WBUR (regional civic coverage)
  - **Historical institutions**: Cambridge Historical Society, Cambridge Public Library archives, Cambridge Historical Commission
  - **Civic organizations**: Cambridge Civic Journal, League of Women Voters Cambridge chapter
- Wikipedia is acceptable as supplementary source for historical facts when paired with authoritative primaries

### Collection visual identity
- Banner image: Cambridge City Hall (neoclassical building — historic and dignified tone)
- Slug: `cambridge-ma`
- The visual should not feel like an ad for MIT or Harvard

### Claude's Discretion
- Exact color theme and banner image styling

- Which civic non-profit work connected to universities is worth including
- How to handle edge cases in question curation

</decisions>
